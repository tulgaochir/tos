Introduction to the Balance Sheet

The Balance Sheet is a financial snapshot of a company's financial position at a specific point in time. It follows the fundamental equation:

Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity

For analysts, the balance sheet is essential for assessing:

üíß

Liquidity

Can the company pay its short-term bills?

üèõÔ∏è

Solvency

Can it meet long-term obligations?

‚ù§Ô∏è

Financial Health

Is the company over-leveraged or under-invested?

Analysts use balance sheet ratios to compare companies and track trends over time.

Structure of the Balance Sheet

A classified balance sheet groups similar items together. Here is a detailed breakdown of its typical structure.

Component Description
Assets (What the company owns)
Current Assets Assets expected to be converted to cash or used up within one year.
Cash & Cash Equivalents The most liquid assets, including currency, bank deposits, and short-term money market instruments.
Marketable Securities Short-term debt or equity investments that are readily marketable.
Accounts Receivable Money owed to the company by customers for goods or services already delivered.
Inventory Raw materials, work-in-process goods, and finished goods held for sale.
Non-Current Assets Long-term assets not expected to be converted to cash within one year.
Property, Plant & Equipment (PP&E) Tangible, long-lived assets used in operations, such as land, buildings, and machinery.
Intangible Assets Non-physical assets like patents, trademarks, and copyrights.
Goodwill Arises from acquiring another company for more than the fair value of its net assets.
Liabilities (What the company owes)
Current Liabilities Obligations due within one year.
Accounts Payable Money owed to suppliers for goods or services received on credit.
Short-Term Debt Loans and other borrowings due within one year.
Accrued Expenses Expenses that have been incurred but not yet paid (e.g., salaries, rent).
Non-Current Liabilities Obligations due after one year.
Long-Term Debt Loans and other borrowings with maturities of more than one year.
Bonds Payable Long-term debt securities issued to investors.
Deferred Tax Liabilities Taxes that will likely be paid in a future period.
Shareholders' Equity (Owners' claim on net assets)
Common Stock / Paid-in Capital The amount invested by shareholders to purchase stock from the company.
Retained Earnings Cumulative net income that has been retained for reinvestment, not paid out as dividends.
Treasury Stock Stock that the company has repurchased from the open market (a contra-equity account).

Goodwill

Understanding Goodwill

Goodwill arises only in a business acquisition. It's the excess of the purchase price over the fair value of the net identifiable assets acquired.

Goodwill = Purchase Price ‚àí Fair Value of Net Identifiable Assets

üìä Accounting Goodwill

Recorded on the balance sheet after an acquisition

üíº Economic Goodwill

Not on the balance sheet ‚Äî reflects brand strength, customer loyalty, or superior management

Post-Acquisition Accounting

  • ‚ùå Not amortized under IFRS or U.S. GAAP
  • üîç Tested for impairment annually
  • üìà Impairment loss is recorded on the income statement as a non-cash expense
  • üìâ Reduces net income, total assets, and ROA

Goodwill Impairment

A company has $50M in goodwill. After a market downturn, the recoverable amount is $40M.

Impairment loss = $50M ‚àí $40M = $10M ‚Üí reduces net income and assets

Analyst Adjustments

Because goodwill is subjective, analysts often adjust financials:

  • ‚Ä¢ Exclude goodwill from total assets when calculating ROA or debt ratios
  • ‚Ä¢ Exclude impairment losses to assess core profitability

Financial Instruments

Classification Based on Business Model and Intent

Under both IFRS 9 and U.S. GAAP, financial assets are classified based on the entity's business model and the contractual cash flow characteristics. Below is a clear comparison of the three primary classifications.

Classification Typical Assets Intent / Business Model Measurement Basis Interest/Dividends Unrealized Gains/Losses
Held-to-Maturity (HTM)
(U.S. GAAP)
Amortized Cost
(IFRS)		 Bonds (debt securities) Hold to maturity; collect contractual cash flows Amortized cost Income Statement n/a ‚Äî No recognition
Trading
(Both U.S. GAAP & IFRS)		 Stocks, bonds, derivatives Active trading for short-term profit Fair Value Through Profit or Loss (FVPL) Income Statement Income Statement
Available-for-Sale (AFS)
(U.S. GAAP)
FVOCI
(IFRS)		 Bonds, equities (non-trading) May sell before maturity, but not actively traded Fair Value Through Other Comprehensive Income (FVOCI) Income Statement Other Comprehensive Income (OCI) ‚Äî Balance Sheet (AOCI)
Note:
  • ‚Ä¢ Under U.S. GAAP, equity investments cannot be classified as HTM (no maturity date)
  • ‚Ä¢ Under IFRS, equity investments can be irrevocably designated as FVOCI (with recycling prohibited)
  • ‚Ä¢ "Available-for-Sale" is not used under IFRS ‚Äî replaced by FVOCI
  • ‚Ä¢ Interest/dividend income is always recognized in P&L regardless of classification

Key Differences Summary

Feature U.S. GAAP IFRS
Equity Investments Generally FVPL unless designated as AFS Can be FVOCI (irrevocable election)
Debt Instruments HTM, AFS, Trading Amortized Cost, FVOCI, FVPL
Reclassification Permitted between AFS and Trading (rare) Generally prohibited
OCI Recycling Yes (for AFS debt) No (for equity FVOCI); Yes (for debt FVOCI)

Bond Classification Decision

A company buys corporate bonds expecting to hold them until maturity to receive interest payments. It does not plan to trade them.

Classification: Held-to-Maturity (U.S. GAAP) or Amortized Cost (IFRS)

Impact: Unrealized price changes do not affect net income ‚Äî only interest income is reported.

Non-Current Liabilities

Non-current liabilities are obligations due beyond one year or the operating cycle.

Long-Term Debt

Includes long-term loans, bonds payable. Usually measured at amortized cost.

Exceptions (measured at fair value):

Held-for-trading liabilities

Derivative liabilities

Hedged non-derivative liabilities

Deferred Tax Liabilities (DTL)

A DTL arises when accounting income > taxable income due to temporary timing differences.

üîç Cause

Lower taxes paid now than reported on income statement

üìä Common Example

Using accelerated depreciation for tax purposes but straight-line for financial reporting

The difference reverses in the future, creating a tax obligation.

Deferred Tax Liability

A company uses straight-line depreciation ($10K/year) for financials but accelerated ($15K/year) for taxes.

  • ‚Ä¢ Year 1: Pre-tax income (financials) = $100K; (tax) = $95K
  • ‚Ä¢ Tax expense (financials) = $25K (25%)
  • ‚Ä¢ Taxes payable (tax return) = $23.75K
  • ‚Ä¢ DTL created = $25K ‚àí $23.75K = $1.25K

Common-Size Analysis and Ratios

Common-Size Balance Sheet

Each item is expressed as a percentage of total assets.

‚ú® Useful for:

  • ‚Ä¢ Tracking changes in asset/liability structure over time
  • ‚Ä¢ Comparing companies of different sizes
  • ‚Ä¢ Identifying business models (e.g., high PPE = manufacturer; high goodwill = acquisitive firm)

Key Balance Sheet Ratios

üíß Liquidity Ratios

Current Ratio
Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities

Interpretation: Higher = better short-term liquidity

Quick Ratio (Acid-Test)
Quick Ratio = (Cash + Marketable Securities + Accounts Receivable) / Current Liabilities

Why? Excludes inventory ‚Äî more conservative measure

Cash Ratio
Cash Ratio = (Cash + Marketable Securities) / Current Liabilities

Most conservative ‚Äî only the most liquid assets included

üèõÔ∏è Solvency Ratios

Debt-to-Equity Ratio
D/E = Total Debt / Total Equity

Critical Formulas - Master These

üìê Essential Balance Sheet Formulas

1. Accounting Equation

Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity

Must always balance! Any transaction affects at least two accounts

2. Current Ratio (Liquidity)

Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities

Benchmark: > 1.0 is healthy; > 2.0 is strong (varies by industry)

3. Quick Ratio (Acid-Test)

Quick Ratio = (Cash + Marketable Securities + A/R) / Current Liabilities

More conservative - excludes inventory. Benchmark: > 1.0

4. Cash Ratio (Most Conservative)

Cash Ratio = (Cash + Marketable Securities) / Current Liabilities

Only most liquid assets. Benchmark: > 0.5 is good

5. Debt-to-Equity Ratio (Solvency)

D/E = Total Debt / Total Shareholders' Equity

Higher ratio = more leverage = more risk. Benchmark varies by industry

6. Goodwill Calculation (Acquisition)

Goodwill = Purchase Price - Fair Value of Net Identifiable Assets

Only arises from acquisition. Not amortized, tested for impairment annually

Common Exam Traps & Mistakes

‚ö†Ô∏è Trap #1: Financial Instruments Classification

Mistake: Thinking unrealized gains on AFS/FVOCI securities flow through the income statement

Reality:

  • Trading: Unrealized gains/losses ‚Üí Income Statement (affects NI)
  • AFS/FVOCI: Unrealized gains/losses ‚Üí OCI (doesn't affect NI until sold)
  • HTM/Amortized Cost: No recognition of unrealized changes

‚ö†Ô∏è Trap #2: Goodwill vs. Intangible Assets

Mistake: Amortizing goodwill over a useful life

Reality:

  • Goodwill: NEVER amortized (under IFRS & U.S. GAAP) - tested for impairment annually
  • Finite-life intangibles: Amortized over useful life
  • Indefinite-life intangibles: Not amortized - tested for impairment

‚ö†Ô∏è Trap #3: Quick Ratio Calculation

Mistake: Including inventory in quick ratio

Reality: Quick ratio = (Cash + Marketable Securities + A/R) / Current Liabilities

Remember: "Quick" assets are those quickly converted to cash - inventory is excluded because it takes time to sell

‚ö†Ô∏è Trap #4: Deferred Tax Liability

Mistake: Confusing when DTL vs. DTA is created

Reality:

  • DTL: Created when accounting income > taxable income (pay more taxes later)
  • DTA: Created when taxable income > accounting income (tax refund later)
  • Common DTL cause: Accelerated depreciation for tax, straight-line for books

‚ö†Ô∏è Trap #5: Internally Generated Intangibles

Mistake: Capitalizing R&D costs under U.S. GAAP

Reality:

  • U.S. GAAP: ALL R&D expensed (including development)
  • IFRS: Research expensed; Development CAN be capitalized if criteria met
  • Both: Internally generated brands/trademarks NEVER capitalized

Practice Questions

Question 1: Current Ratio Calculation

A company has the following: Cash $50,000; A/R $80,000; Inventory $120,000; PP&E $400,000; Current Liabilities $150,000. What is the current ratio?

A) 1.67
B) 0.87
C) 4.33
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: A) 1.67

Calculation:

  1. Current Assets = Cash + A/R + Inventory = $50K + $80K + $120K = $250K
  2. PP&E is NOT a current asset (excluded)
  3. Current Ratio = $250K / $150K = 1.67

Interpretation: For every $1 of current liabilities, the company has $1.67 of current assets

Question 2: Quick Ratio

Using the same data from Question 1, what is the quick ratio?

A) 1.67
B) 0.87
C) 1.20
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B) 0.87

Calculation:

  1. Quick Assets = Cash + Marketable Securities + A/R = $50K + $0 + $80K = $130K
  2. Inventory is EXCLUDED from quick ratio
  3. Quick Ratio = $130K / $150K = 0.87

Note: Quick ratio < 1.0 suggests potential liquidity concerns if inventory cannot be quickly converted to cash

Question 3: Goodwill Calculation

Company A acquires Company B for $10 million. Company B's identifiable assets have a fair value of $8 million and liabilities of $3 million. What is the goodwill recorded?

A) $2 million
B) $5 million
C) $10 million
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B) $5 million

Step-by-step:

  1. Fair Value of Net Identifiable Assets = Assets - Liabilities = $8M - $3M = $5M
  2. Goodwill = Purchase Price - FV of Net Assets = $10M - $5M = $5M

Key: Goodwill = excess paid over fair value of NET (assets minus liabilities)

Question 4: Financial Instruments - Unrealized Gains

A company holds equity securities classified as Trading. The securities increase in value by $100,000 during the year but are not sold. How is this treated?

A) No recognition until sold
B) Recognized in Other Comprehensive Income
C) Recognized in Net Income
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: C) Recognized in Net Income

Financial Instrument Classification Rules:

  • Trading Securities: ALL gains/losses (realized & unrealized) ‚Üí Income Statement
  • AFS/FVOCI: Unrealized gains ‚Üí OCI; Realized gains ‚Üí Income Statement
  • HTM: No recognition of unrealized gains/losses

Impact: Trading classification increases reported net income by $100K (even though no cash received)

Question 5: Intangible Assets Treatment

Under IFRS, a company spends $2 million on development costs that meet capitalization criteria. How should this be treated under IFRS vs. U.S. GAAP?

A) Both capitalize
B) IFRS capitalizes; U.S. GAAP expenses
C) Both expense
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B) IFRS capitalizes; U.S. GAAP expenses

Key Difference:

  • IFRS: Research = expensed; Development = can be capitalized if criteria met (technical feasibility, intention to complete, etc.)
  • U.S. GAAP: ALL R&D costs expensed (except software development after technological feasibility)

Impact: IFRS company reports higher assets and higher Year 1 net income (only amortization, not full expense)

Question 6: Deferred Tax Liability

A company uses straight-line depreciation ($10,000/year) for financial reporting and accelerated depreciation ($15,000/year) for tax purposes. Pre-tax book income is $100,000. Tax rate is 30%. What is the deferred tax liability created in Year 1?

A) $1,500
B) $5,000
C) $30,000
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: A) $1,500

Calculation:

  1. Book income = $100,000; Taxable income = $100,000 - $5,000 extra depreciation = $95,000
  2. Temporary difference = $15,000 - $10,000 = $5,000
  3. DTL = Temporary difference √ó Tax rate = $5,000 √ó 30% = $1,500

Why DTL? Company pays less tax now ($28,500 vs. $30,000) but will pay more later when depreciation reverses
