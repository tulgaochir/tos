Free Cash Flow (FCF)

Free cash flow is the cash available after essential reinvestment. It's crucial for valuation.

Free Cash Flow to the Firm (FCFF)

The cash available to all investors (debt and equity holders).

FCFF = CFO + Interest √ó (1 ‚àí Tax Rate) ‚àí FCInv

Or from Net Income:

FCFF = NI + NCC + Interest √ó (1 ‚àí Tax Rate) ‚àí FCInv ‚àí WCInv

Where: NI = Net Income, NCC = Non-Cash Charges, FCInv = Fixed Capital Investment, WCInv = Working Capital Investment.

FCFF Example

CFO = $500K, Interest = $50K, Tax Rate = 30%, FCInv = $120K

After-tax interest = $50K √ó (1 ‚àí 0.30) = $35K

FCFF = $500K + $35K ‚àí $120K = $415K

Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE)

The cash available to common shareholders after all obligations.

FCFE = CFO ‚àí FCInv + Net Borrowing

Or from Net Income:

FCFE = NI + NCC ‚àí FCInv ‚àí WCInv + Net Borrowing

Net Borrowing = New Debt Issued ‚àí Debt Repaid

FCFE Example

CFO = $500K, FCInv = $120K, New Debt = $80K, Debt Repaid = $50K

Net Borrowing = $80K ‚àí $50K = $30K

FCFE = $500K ‚àí $120K + $30K = $410K

Key Cash Flow Ratios

Ratios help assess performance, risk, and sustainability.

Performance Ratios

Cash Flow to Revenue

CFO / Net Revenue

Purpose: Measures how much operating cash is generated per dollar of sales.

Example: CFO = $150K, Revenue = $1M ‚Üí Ratio = 15% ‚Üí $0.15 cash per $1 of revenue.

Cash Return on Assets (CROA)

CFO / Average Total Assets

Purpose: Measures cash efficiency of asset investment.

Example: CFO = $150K, Avg Assets = $2M ‚Üí 7.5%

Cash Return on Equity (CROE)

CFO / Average Shareholders' Equity

Purpose: Measures cash return to owners.

Example: CFO = $150K, Avg Equity = $800K ‚Üí 18.75%

Cash to Income

CFO / Operating Income

Purpose: Assesses quality of earnings.

Example: CFO = $150K, Operating Income = $140K ‚Üí Ratio = 1.07 ‚Üí strong conversion.

Cash Flow per Share

(CFO ‚àí Preferred Dividends) / Weighted Avg Shares

Purpose: Operating cash flow on a per-share basis.

Example: CFO = $150K, Pref Div = $10K, Shares = 100K ‚Üí $1.40/share

Coverage Ratios

Debt Coverage

CFO / Total Debt

Purpose: Ability to cover total debt with operating cash.

Example: CFO = $150K, Debt = $500K ‚Üí 30%

Interest Coverage

(CFO + Interest Paid + Taxes Paid) / Interest Paid

Purpose: Ability to meet interest obligations.

Example: CFO = $150K, Int = $30K, Taxes = $40K ‚Üí ($220K)/$30K = 7.33x

Reinvestment Ratio

CFO / Cash Paid for Long-Term Assets

Purpose: Can operations fund asset purchases?

Example: CFO = $150K, CapEx = $100K ‚Üí 1.5x

Debt Payment Coverage

CFO / Debt Repayments

Purpose: Ability to pay down debt.

Example: CFO = $150K, Debt Paid = $75K ‚Üí 2.0x

Dividend Payment Coverage

CFO / Dividends Paid

Purpose: Sustainability of dividend payouts.

Example: CFO = $150K, Dividends = $50K ‚Üí 3.0x

Investing & Financing Coverage

CFO / (Investing + Financing Outflows)

Purpose: Can operations fund all external outflows?

Example: CFO = $150K, Outflows = $180K ‚Üí 0.83 ‚Üí deficit

Critical Formulas - Master These

1. FCFF (Free Cash Flow to the Firm)

FCFF = CFO + Interest √ó (1 ‚àí Tax Rate) ‚àí FCInv

Alternative: FCFF = NI + NCC + Interest √ó (1 ‚àí T) ‚àí FCInv ‚àí WCInv

2. FCFE (Free Cash Flow to Equity)

FCFE = CFO ‚àí FCInv + Net Borrowing

Alternative: FCFE = NI + NCC ‚àí FCInv ‚àí WCInv + Net Borrowing

3. Converting FCFF to FCFE

FCFE = FCFF ‚àí Interest √ó (1 ‚àí Tax Rate) + Net Borrowing

4. Cash Flow to Revenue Ratio

CFO / Net Revenue

Higher is better; consistent ratios indicate quality earnings

5. Cash Return on Assets (CROA)

6. Debt Coverage Ratio

CFO / Total Debt

Higher ratios indicate better ability to service debt

Common Exam Traps & Mistakes

Trap 1: Forgetting the After-Tax Interest Adjustment

When calculating FCFF from CFO, remember to add back after-tax interest, not gross interest. Formula: Interest √ó (1 ‚àí Tax Rate). Many candidates forget the (1 ‚àí T) component.

Trap 2: Confusing Net Borrowing with Total Debt

Net Borrowing = New Debt Issued ‚àí Debt Repaid. Don't use total debt outstanding. Also, don't confuse cash interest paid with interest expense on the income statement.

Trap 3: Misclassifying Working Capital Changes

An increase in working capital is a USE of cash (negative). Decrease in working capital is a SOURCE of cash (positive). Signs matter!

Trap 4: Using Wrong Average for Ratios

For CROA and CROE, use the average of beginning and ending balances. (Beginning + Ending) / 2. Don't use just ending balance.

Trap 5: Ignoring Cash Flow Quality Signals

High net income but low CFO is a red flag. Also watch for: declining CFO/Revenue ratio, negative CFO in mature companies, and CFO consistently less than Net Income.

Memory Aids & Mnemonics

FCFF vs FCFE - "FIRM vs EQUITY"

FCFF (Firm): Available to ALL investors (debt + equity)

FCFE (Equity): Available ONLY to equity holders

To go from Firm to Equity: subtract after-tax interest, add net borrowing

"COIN" for FCF Components

C - Cash from Operations (CFO)

O - Out goes FCInv (Fixed Capital Investment)

I - Interest adjustment (for FCFF)

N - Net borrowing (for FCFE)

Coverage Ratios - "Higher is Better"

All coverage ratios: Higher values = Stronger financial position

‚Ä¢ Debt Coverage > 0.20 (20%) is healthy

‚Ä¢ Reinvestment > 1.0 means CFO covers CapEx

‚Ä¢ Dividend Coverage > 1.5 suggests sustainable payouts

Quality Check: "NIC" Test

N - Net Income vs CFO (should be close)

I - Is CFO consistently positive?

C - Cash to Income ratio near 1.0 or higher

Practice Questions

Question 1: A company reports CFO of $800K, interest expense of $100K (tax rate 30%), and FCInv of $250K. What is FCFF?

Show Solution

Step 1: Calculate after-tax interest = $100K √ó (1 ‚àí 0.30) = $70K

Step 2: FCFF = CFO + After-tax Interest ‚àí FCInv

Step 3: FCFF = $800K + $70K ‚àí $250K = $620K

Key: Don't forget to adjust interest for taxes!

Question 2: CFO = $500K, FCInv = $200K, New Debt = $150K, Debt Repaid = $100K. Calculate FCFE.

Show Solution

Step 1: Net Borrowing = $150K ‚àí $100K = $50K

Step 2: FCFE = CFO ‚àí FCInv + Net Borrowing

Step 3: FCFE = $500K ‚àí $200K + $50K = $350K

Question 3: Company A has Net Income of $200K and CFO of $150K. Company B has Net Income of $200K and CFO of $220K. Which has higher quality earnings?

Show Solution

Answer: Company B

Company A: Cash to Income = $150K / $200K = 0.75 (75%)

Company B: Cash to Income = $220K / $200K = 1.10 (110%)

Company B converts more than 100% of net income to operating cash, indicating high-quality earnings. Company A may have aggressive accruals or working capital issues.

Question 4: FCFF = $500K, Interest = $80K, Tax Rate = 25%, Net Borrowing = $40K. Calculate FCFE.

Show Solution

Step 1: After-tax Interest = $80K √ó (1 ‚àí 0.25) = $60K

Step 2: FCFE = FCFF ‚àí After-tax Interest + Net Borrowing

Step 3: FCFE = $500K ‚àí $60K + $40K = $480K

Question 5: CFO = $600K, Total Debt = $2M, Dividends Paid = $150K. Calculate debt coverage and dividend coverage ratios.

Show Solution

Debt Coverage: $600K / $2,000K = 0.30 or 30%

Dividend Coverage: $600K / $150K = 4.0√ó

Interpretation: Strong dividend coverage (4.0√ó is healthy), and CFO covers 30% of total debt annually (reasonable for most industries).

Question 6: CFO = $400K, Revenue = $2.5M, Beginning Assets = $3M, Ending Assets = $3.4M. Calculate CFO to Revenue and CROA.

Show Solution

CFO to Revenue: $400K / $2,500K = 0.16 or 16%

Average Assets: ($3M + $3.4M) / 2 = $3.2M

CROA: $400K / $3,200K = 0.125 or 12.5%

The company generates $0.16 in cash for every $1 of revenue and 12.5% cash return on assets.

Study Tips for 90th Percentile Performance

  • 1. Master the Formula Variations: Practice calculating FCFF and FCFE starting from both CFO and Net Income. Exam questions often mix these approaches.
  • 2. Create a Formula Card: Write all FCF formulas on one card. Practice deriving one from another (e.g., FCFE from FCFF).
  • 3. Practice Under Time Pressure: Set a 2-minute timer for each FCF calculation problem. You need speed AND accuracy.
  • 4. Understand the "Why": Don't just memorize. Understand WHY we add back after-tax interest for FCFF (it's cash available to all investors) and WHY we add net borrowing for FCFE (it's a source of cash for equity).
  • 5. Watch for Units: Pay attention to thousands vs. millions in exam questions. A simple decimal error can cost you points.
  • 6. Link to Other Topics: Connect cash flow analysis to equity valuation (FCF is used in DCF models) and to credit analysis (coverage ratios assess default risk).
  • 7. Common-Size Practice: Create a common-size cash flow statement for a real company. This builds intuition for what "normal" looks like.
