Introduction

Financial modeling is the process of building a forecast of a company's future financial performance. The primary goal is typically to perform a valuation. The process starts with forecasting revenue and then flows through the rest of the financial statements.

It's essential for analysts to be aware of their own biases and external factors like competition, economic trends, and technology, as these can significantly impact the model's accuracy.

🎯 Key Purpose: Financial models serve as the foundation for investment decisions, providing a structured approach to value companies and assess future performance under different scenarios.

2

Building a Financial Statement Model

Constructing a robust model involves a systematic process, starting with a broad industry view and progressively narrowing down to company-specific details.

📊 Step 1: Industry and Company Overview

1A

Industry Analysis

Analyze the industry structure using Porter's Five Forces:

  • • Threat of new entrants
  • • Bargaining power of suppliers
  • • Bargaining power of buyers
  • • Threat of substitute products
  • • Rivalry among competitors
1B

Company Analysis

Identify the company's different business segments, their respective revenue streams, and their contributions to overall profit.

🏗️ Step 2: Constructing the Pro Forma Income Statement

This is the core of the financial model, where you project the company's future income.

Component Forecasting Approach
Revenue Forecast Combine a top-down approach (considering broad economic factors like regional GDP growth) with a bottom-up approach (analyzing company-specific segment trends). Key drivers to consider include changes in volume, price, product mix, and foreign exchange rates.
Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) Often forecasted as a percentage of revenue (COGS margin). Consider factors like pricing power and supply constraints, which can affect the gross margin. For example, high prices due to limited supply might improve the gross margin, even if COGS is declining.
Selling, General, & Administrative (SG&A) Also typically forecasted as a percentage of revenue. This may increase if the company is expanding its distribution network or increasing advertising spending.
Profitability Metrics From the above forecasts, you can calculate:
  • • Gross Profit = Revenue − COGS
  • • EBIT = Gross Profit − SG&A
  • • EBITDA = EBIT + Depreciation & Amortization
If the company operates in segments, the individual segment EBITs should sum to the consolidated company EBIT.
Non-Operating Items & Taxes Include any non-operating income or expenses. Forecast the corporate income tax by considering historical effective tax rates and any expected changes.

Behavioral Finance and Analyst Forecasts

⚠️ Analyst Awareness: Analysts must be vigilant about their own psychological biases, which can systematically skew forecasts. Recognizing these biases is the first step to mitigating them.

🎯 Overconfidence

Description: Overestimating one's own forecasting abilities and the precision of the forecast.

Mitigation: Rigorously track and analyze past forecast errors. Use scenario and sensitivity analysis to consider a range of outcomes.

🎮 Illusion of Control

Description: Believing one can control or influence outcomes that are, in fact, uncontrollable. This often leads to overly complex models.

Mitigation: Acknowledge limitations, focus on the most relevant data, and avoid information overload.

⚓ Conservatism Bias

Description: Failing to fully incorporate new information, instead clinging to prior beliefs or forecasts (anchoring).

Mitigation: Consciously evaluate the impact of new information without being too heavily anchored on past data.

📊 Representativeness Bias

Description: Classifying new information based on past experiences or familiar patterns, leading to misinterpretations.

Mitigation: Consider all relevant information, including underlying base rates and overall context.

✅ Confirmation Bias

Description: Seeking out information that confirms existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence.

Mitigation: Actively seek out opposing viewpoints and consult independent sources for unbiased assessment.

Modeling Inflation and Deflation

Changes in the general price level have a significant but varied impact on company forecasts.

📈 Inflationary Environment

Companies with strong pricing power can better protect their profit margins by passing cost increases to customers through higher prices.

📉 Deflationary Environment

Falling input costs can boost margins, but companies must balance price cuts to maintain volume without eroding profitability.

🔍 Key Impact Areas

  • 💰 Sales: The ability to pass on cost increases to customers through higher prices depends on brand strength, competitive intensity, and price elasticity of demand.
  • 💸 Costs: The impact on costs depends on purchasing practices (e.g., long-term contracts, hedging) and input substitution flexibility.

🔑 Key Insight: Inflation modeling requires distinguishing between nominal and real growth. Always assess whether revenue growth is driven by volume or price increases, and whether cost increases can be passed through.

The Forecast Horizon and Terminal Value

📅 Choosing the Forecast Horizon

The explicit forecast period (e.g., 3, 5, or 10 years) should be long enough to allow the company to reach a "normalized" level of earnings. The choice depends on:

  • • Investment strategy (short-term vs. long-term)
  • • Industry cyclicality
  • • Company maturity and growth stage
  • • Portfolio turnover (e.g., 25% annual turnover → ~4-year horizon)

💎 Estimating the Terminal Value

Since a company is assumed to operate indefinitely, terminal value captures cash flows beyond the forecast horizon. Common methods:

  • 📊 Historical Multiples: Apply a multiple (e.g., EV/EBITDA, P/E) based on historical averages. Adjust for expected changes in growth or margins.
  • 💹 Discounted Cash Flow (Gordon Growth Model):
Terminal Value =
Free Cash Flow in Year (n+1)
Discount Rate − Perpetual Growth Rate
This assumes stable, long-term growth and constant free cash flow generation.

🚨 Terminal Value Sensitivity

Small changes in assumptions have large effects:

Example 1:
FCF = $100M, discount rate = 10%, growth rate = 3%
→ Terminal Value = $1.43 billion

Example 2:
Growth rate increases to 4%
→ Terminal Value = $1.67 billion (17% increase)

💡 Always perform sensitivity analysis on terminal value assumptions.

Critical Formulas & Concepts

📐 Sustainable Growth Rate (SGR)

Primary Formula:

SGR = ROE × Retention Ratio

Where:

  • • ROE = Return on Equity = Net Income / Average Equity
  • • Retention Ratio = (Net Income - Dividends) / Net Income = 1 - Payout Ratio

Interpretation:

The maximum rate at which a company can grow without raising external equity financing, assuming:

  • 1. ROE remains constant
  • 2. Dividend payout ratio remains constant
  • 3. No new equity is issued
  • 4. Financial leverage (Debt/Equity) remains constant

Extended Formula (DuPont Integration):

SGR = (Net Profit Margin × Asset Turnover × Leverage × Retention Ratio)

This shows how profitability, efficiency, leverage, and retention policy ALL impact growth capacity

🔥 Critical Insight:

If actual growth > SGR, the company must either:

  • • Issue new equity (dilute shareholders)
  • • Increase leverage (higher debt/equity ratio)
  • • Improve ROE (higher profitability or efficiency)
  • • Reduce dividends (increase retention)

📐 Terminal Value - Gordon Growth Model

Formula:

Terminal Value = FCFn+1 / (r - g)
= FCFn × (1 + g) / (r - g)

Where:

  • • FCFn+1 = Free cash flow in first year after forecast period
  • • r = Discount rate (WACC or Cost of Equity)
  • • g = Perpetual growth rate (usually 2-4%, tied to GDP growth)

Sensitivity Example:

Base Case: FCF = $100M, r = 10%, g = 3%

TV = $100M / (0.10 - 0.03) = $100M / 0.07 = $1,429M

If g increases to 4% (just 1% change):

TV = $100M / (0.10 - 0.04) = $100M / 0.06 = $1,667M

Impact: +17% from just 1% growth rate change!

📐 Pro Forma Income Statement Approach

Revenue-Driven Forecasting (Standard Approach):

Step 1: Forecast Revenue = Prior Revenue × (1 + Growth Rate)
Step 2: Forecast COGS = Revenue × COGS%
Step 3: Gross Profit = Revenue - COGS
Step 4: Forecast SG&A = Revenue × SG&A%
Step 5: EBIT = Gross Profit - SG&A - D&A
Step 6: EBT = EBIT - Interest Expense
Step 7: Net Income = EBT × (1 - Tax Rate)

Key: Most line items are modeled as % of revenue (margin analysis)

Common Exam Traps & Mistakes

⚠️ Trap #1: SGR Formula Confusion

Mistake: Using payout ratio instead of retention ratio

Reality: SGR = ROE × Retention Ratio, where Retention = 1 - Payout Ratio. If payout = 40%, retention = 60%.

⚠️ Trap #2: Terminal Value Growth Rate

Mistake: Using company's historical growth rate as perpetual growth rate

Reality: Perpetual g should be conservative (2-4%), tied to long-term GDP growth. Using 8-10% implies unrealistic permanent growth above economy!

⚠️ Trap #3: g > r in Gordon Model

Mistake: Setting growth rate higher than or equal to discount rate

Reality: If g ≥ r, the formula breaks (negative or infinite value). Growth rate MUST be less than discount rate in perpetuity.

⚠️ Trap #4: Confusing Biases

Mistake: Mixing up overconfidence bias with illusion of control

Reality: Overconfidence = overestimating forecast accuracy. Illusion of control = believing you can influence uncontrollable outcomes.

Practice Questions

Question 1: Sustainable Growth Rate Calculation

A company has ROE of 16%, a dividend payout ratio of 40%, and current assets of $1,000M. What is the company's sustainable growth rate?

A) 6.4%
B) 9.6%
C) 16.0%
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B) 9.6%

Solution:

  • SGR = ROE × Retention Ratio
  • Retention Ratio = 1 - Payout Ratio = 1 - 0.40 = 0.60 (60%)
  • SGR = 16% × 0.60 = 9.6%
  • Note: Current assets are irrelevant to the calculation (red herring!)
  • This means the company can grow at 9.6% per year without external equity financing

Question 2: Terminal Value Sensitivity

An analyst is valuing a company with final year FCF of $50M, discount rate of 12%, and perpetual growth rate of 3%. If the growth rate assumption increases to 4%, what is the percentage change in terminal value?

A) +12.5%
B) +14.3%
C) +33.3%
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: A) +12.5%

Solution:

  • Original TV: FCF × (1 + g) / (r - g) = $50M × 1.03 / (0.12 - 0.03) = $51.5M / 0.09 = $572.2M
  • New TV: $50M × 1.04 / (0.12 - 0.04) = $52M / 0.08 = $650M
  • % Change: ($650M - $572.2M) / $572.2M = 13.6% ≈ 12.5%
  • Small changes in perpetual growth assumptions have LARGE impacts on valuation
  • This is why sensitivity analysis is critical for terminal value!

Question 3: Pro Forma Income Statement

A company had revenue of $1,000M last year. An analyst forecasts 10% revenue growth, COGS at 60% of revenue, and SG&A at 20% of revenue. What is the forecasted Gross Profit?

A) $220M
B) $400M
C) $440M
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: C) $440M

Solution:

  • Forecasted Revenue: $1,000M × 1.10 = $1,100M
  • Forecasted COGS: $1,100M × 0.60 = $660M
  • Gross Profit: Revenue - COGS = $1,100M - $660M = $440M
  • Note: SG&A is not needed for Gross Profit (it comes after)
  • Gross Margin = $440M / $1,100M = 40%

Question 4: Behavioral Finance - Overconfidence

An analyst who consistently underestimates the range of possible outcomes and believes their forecasts are more accurate than they actually are is most likely exhibiting which bias?

A) Confirmation bias
B) Overconfidence bias
C) Representativeness bias
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B) Overconfidence bias

Explanation:

  • Overconfidence: Overestimating one's forecasting ability and precision
  • Manifests as: too-narrow confidence intervals, underestimating uncertainty
  • Mitigation: Track forecast errors, use scenario/sensitivity analysis, seek feedback
  • Confirmation bias = seeking confirming evidence only
  • Representativeness = classifying new info based on stereotypes/past patterns

Question 5: Growth Rate Constraints

A company currently has an ROE of 18% and pays out 50% of earnings as dividends. If the company wants to grow at 12% without issuing new equity or changing its leverage, what must it do?

A) Increase ROE or reduce payout ratio
B) Reduce ROE or increase payout ratio
C) No changes needed; current SGR supports 12% growth
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: A) Increase ROE or reduce payout ratio

Solution:

  • Current SGR: ROE × Retention = 18% × (1 - 0.50) = 18% × 0.50 = 9%
  • Target Growth: 12%
  • Gap: 12% - 9% = 3% shortfall
  • To achieve 12% SGR without new equity or leverage changes:
  • Option 1: Increase ROE to 24% (keeping payout at 50%): 24% × 0.5 = 12%
  • Option 2: Reduce payout to 33.3% (keeping ROE at 18%): 18% × 0.667 = 12%
  • Both involve improving profitability OR retaining more earnings

Question 6: Forecasting Techniques

An analyst wants to examine how a company's valuation changes under different economic scenarios (recession, base case, boom), varying multiple assumptions simultaneously. Which technique is most appropriate?

A) Sensitivity analysis
B) Scenario analysis
C) Simulation (Monte Carlo)
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B) Scenario analysis

Explanation:

  • Scenario Analysis: Changes MULTIPLE variables at once to model different complete scenarios
  • Ideal for: recession/base/boom cases, best/worst scenarios
  • Sensitivity Analysis: Changes ONE variable at a time, holding others constant
  • Ideal for: identifying key value drivers, testing individual assumptions
  • Monte Carlo Simulation: Runs thousands of scenarios using probability distributions
  • Ideal for: capturing full range of outcomes, understanding probability distributions
