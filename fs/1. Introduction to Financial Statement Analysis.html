Introduction to Financial Analysis

Financial analysis is the process of using a company's financial data to assess its performance, financial health, and future prospects. It's essential for making informed investment, lending, and management decisions.

Who Uses Financial Analysis?

Different stakeholders have different goals:

üè¶ Debt Investors (Creditors)

Banks, bondholders, and lenders. Their main concern is whether the company can repay its debt on time.

  • ‚Ä¢ Focus on liquidity (short-term cash flow)
  • ‚Ä¢ Focus on solvency (long-term ability to meet obligations)
  • ‚Ä¢ Key questions: Can the company pay interest? Will it repay the principal?

üìà Equity Investors (Shareholders)

Owners of the company. They care about profitability, growth, and long-term value creation.

  • ‚Ä¢ Want rising earnings and strong returns on capital
  • ‚Ä¢ Desire sustainable dividends
  • ‚Ä¢ Key questions: Is the stock undervalued? Can the company grow earnings?

Different Perspectives

A company reports strong profits but has high debt and weak cash flow.

üî¥ Creditors may avoid it

Too risky to repay loans

üü¢ Equity investors might still buy

If they believe profits will grow and debt can be managed

The Financial Statement Analysis Framework

A structured, six-step framework ensures consistent and thorough analysis. This is the gold standard used by CFA charterholders and professional analysts.

1

Define the Purpose and Context

  • ‚Ä¢ Why are you analyzing the company? (Investment, credit, M&A?)
  • ‚Ä¢ Define the output: report, presentation, or internal memo
  • ‚Ä¢ Set time constraints and data availability
2

Collect Input Data

  • ‚Ä¢ Primary sources: Financial statements (I/S, B/S, SCF, Statement of Shareholders' Equity)
  • ‚Ä¢ Secondary sources: Industry reports, economic data, competitor filings
  • ‚Ä¢ Use reliable platforms: SEC EDGAR, Bloomberg, company websites
3

Process the Data

  • ‚Ä¢ Calculate financial ratios (profitability, liquidity, leverage, efficiency)
  • ‚Ä¢ Create common-size statements (e.g., income statement as % of revenue)
  • ‚Ä¢ Perform trend analysis and regression if needed
4

Analyze and Interpret

  • ‚Ä¢ Turn processed data into insights
  • ‚Ä¢ Compare results to peers, historical performance, and industry benchmarks
  • ‚Ä¢ Identify strengths, weaknesses, risks, and growth drivers
5

Develop and Communicate Conclusions

  • ‚Ä¢ Form clear conclusions: "The stock is undervalued," "Credit risk is high"
  • ‚Ä¢ Present findings in a well-structured report
  • ‚Ä¢ Disclose limitations and risks ‚Äî required by CFA Institute Code of Ethics
6

Follow Up

  • ‚Ä¢ Financial analysis is not a one-time task
  • ‚Ä¢ Update your analysis with new data (quarterly earnings, economic shifts)
  • ‚Ä¢ Revise recommendations as needed

Comparison of IFRS and U.S. GAAP

Most countries use IFRS, while U.S. public companies use U.S. GAAP. Analysts must understand key differences because they affect financial statements and comparability.

Key Differences Between IFRS and U.S. GAAP

Topic U.S. GAAP IFRS
Inventory Valuation Allows LIFO (Last-In, First-Out) Prohibits LIFO ‚Äî only FIFO and weighted average allowed
Extraordinary Items Separately reported on income statement (rare) No segregation ‚Äî all items part of normal operations
Development Costs Expensed as incurred (reduces profit) Can be capitalized if criteria met (e.g., technical feasibility)
Inventory Write-Down Reversals Not allowed ‚Äî once written down, stays down Allowed if market value recovers

‚ö° Why These Differences Matter

  • ‚Ä¢ LIFO vs. FIFO: In inflation, LIFO increases COGS and lowers profit ‚Üí lower taxes but lower reported earnings
  • ‚Ä¢ Development costs: Capitalizing R&D under IFRS increases assets and future profits; expensing under GAAP reduces current profit
  • ‚Ä¢ Write-down reversals: IFRS allows profit boosts if inventory value recovers ‚Äî can be used to manage earnings

Stay Updated on Standard Changes

Accounting standards evolve. The IASB, FASB, and CFA Institute regularly issue updates. Analysts must monitor changes to understand their impact on financials and valuations.

Common Exam Traps & Mistakes

‚ö†Ô∏è Trap #1: Confusing Standard-Setters with Regulators

Mistake: Thinking the SEC sets accounting standards

Reality: FASB sets standards (U.S. GAAP), IASB sets IFRS. The SEC enforces compliance but doesn't create standards.

‚ö†Ô∏è Trap #2: LIFO Availability

Mistake: Thinking LIFO is allowed under IFRS

Reality: LIFO is ONLY allowed under U.S. GAAP, NOT under IFRS

‚ö†Ô∏è Trap #3: Development Costs Treatment

Mistake: Capitalizing development costs under U.S. GAAP

Reality: U.S. GAAP expenses all R&D. IFRS allows capitalization of development costs (not research) if criteria are met.

‚ö†Ô∏è Trap #4: Audit Opinion Implications

Mistake: Treating qualified and adverse opinions as similar

Reality: Qualified = issue exists but overall statements are fair. Adverse = statements are materially misstated (worst opinion)

Practice Questions

Question 1: Framework Application

An analyst is evaluating whether to recommend a buy or sell on Company XYZ's stock. In the financial statement analysis framework, this task occurs in which step?

A) Define the purpose and context
B) Process the data
C) Develop and communicate conclusions
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: C) Develop and communicate conclusions

Making a buy/sell recommendation is part of forming conclusions based on the analysis. Step A (Define purpose) would establish that you're doing equity valuation, but the actual recommendation comes at the conclusion stage.

Question 2: IFRS vs. U.S. GAAP

Which of the following is permitted under IFRS but NOT under U.S. GAAP?

A) Use of LIFO inventory valuation
B) Reversal of inventory write-downs
C) Expensing of research costs
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B) Reversal of inventory write-downs

Explanation:

  • IFRS allows write-down reversals if inventory value recovers
  • U.S. GAAP prohibits reversals - once written down, it stays down
  • A is wrong: LIFO is allowed under U.S. GAAP but prohibited under IFRS
  • C is wrong: Both IFRS and U.S. GAAP require research costs to be expensed

Question 3: SEC Filings

A U.S. public company announces that its CEO has resigned unexpectedly. Which SEC filing would the company most likely use to report this event?

A) Form 10-K
B) Form 10-Q
C) Form 8-K
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: C) Form 8-K

Form 8-K is used to report material events that occur between regular reporting periods, such as:

  • Changes in senior management (CEO, CFO, directors)
  • Mergers and acquisitions
  • Bankruptcy filings
  • Major asset dispositions

10-K is annual, 10-Q is quarterly - both too infrequent for urgent news.

Question 4: Audit Opinions

An auditor issues a qualified opinion on a company's financial statements. This most likely indicates that:

A) The financial statements contain material misstatements that are pervasive
B) The auditor was unable to gather sufficient evidence to form an opinion
C) There are some issues, but the statements are overall fairly presented
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: C) There are some issues, but the statements are overall fairly presented

Remember the hierarchy:

  • Unqualified: Clean opinion, no issues
  • Qualified: "Except for" certain issues, statements are fair (Answer C)
  • Adverse: Material misstatements are pervasive (Answer A describes this)
  • Disclaimer: Insufficient evidence (Answer B describes this)

Question 5: Standard-Setting Bodies

Which organization is responsible for setting International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS)?

A) International Accounting Standards Board (IASB)
B) International Organization of Securities Commissions (IOSCO)
C) European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA)
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: A) International Accounting Standards Board (IASB)

Remember the distinction:

  • Standard-Setters (create rules): IASB creates IFRS, FASB creates U.S. GAAP
  • Regulators (enforce rules): IOSCO and ESMA are regulatory bodies
