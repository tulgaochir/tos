Structure of the Income Statement

Income statements can be presented in a single-step format (Total Revenues - Total Expenses = Net Income) or a multi-step format. The multi-step format is more detailed and useful for analysis, providing key subtotals.

Typical Multi-Step Structure

Component Description & Formula
Revenue (or Sales) Top-line earnings from selling goods or services.
(-) Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) Direct costs attributable to the production of goods sold.
= Gross Profit Revenue - COGS. Shows profit from core production/service activities before other expenses.
(-) Operating Expenses Costs not directly tied to production (e.g., Selling, General & Admin (SG&A), R&D, Depreciation).
= Operating Income (EBIT) Gross Profit - Operating Expenses. Profit from normal business operations. A key measure of core profitability.
(+/-) Non-Operating Items Income or expenses from outside the core business (e.g., Interest Expense, Interest Income, Gains/Losses on investments).
= Earnings Before Tax (EBT) Operating Income +/- Non-Operating Items. Profit before the effect of taxes.
(-) Income Tax Expense Provision for income taxes.
= Net Income EBT - Taxes. The "bottom line" profit attributable to all shareholders after all expenses.

Revenue Recognition

Under accrual accounting, revenue is recognized when earned ‚Äî when goods/services are delivered ‚Äî not when cash is received.

Key Scenarios

üí≥ Sale on Credit

  • ‚Ä¢ Deliver product ‚Üí recognize revenue
  • ‚Ä¢ Create Accounts Receivable (asset)

üí∞ Cash in Advance

  • ‚Ä¢ Receive cash ‚Üí create Deferred Revenue (liability)
  • ‚Ä¢ Recognize revenue over time as service is delivered

2

3

Total consideration expected

4

Based on standalone selling prices

5

Recognize revenue

When control is transferred

  • ‚Ä¢ Two performance obligations
  • ‚Ä¢ $500 revenue recognized when phone is delivered
  • ‚Ä¢ $700 recognized over 24 months ($29.17/month)

Capitalizing vs. Expensing

Should a cost be expensed now or capitalized as an asset? The decision hinges on whether the cost provides future economic benefit beyond the current period.

Financial Item Capitalizing Expensing
Recognition Rule Future benefit > 1 period + reliably measurable cost ‚Üí Capitalize Benefit consumed immediately or not reliably measurable ‚Üí Expense
Year 1 Net Income Higher (only depreciation/amortization hits P&L) Lower (full expense recognized immediately)
Future Net Income Lower in subsequent periods (due to depreciation/amortization) No future impact
Balance Sheet (Assets) Higher initially; declines over time due to depreciation Lower (no asset created)
Operating Cash Flow (OCF) No immediate impact (non-cash expense later) Decreases immediately (cash outflow classified as operating)
Investing Cash Flow (ICF) Decreases (cash outflow classified as investing) No impact

Practical Example: $100,000 Capitalized Asset

Assume a $100,000 cost is capitalized and depreciated straight-line over 5 years ($20,000/year):

  • ‚Ä¢ Year 1 Income Statement: -$20,000 (pre-tax)
  • ‚Ä¢ Year 1 Cash Flow: OCF unchanged; ICF -$100,000
  • ‚Ä¢ If Expensed: Year 1 Income Statement: -$100,000; OCF -$100,000; ICF unchanged

üí° Capitalizing smooths earnings and shifts cash flow from investing to operating over time.

Key Capitalization Rules

üèóÔ∏è Interest Costs

Capitalized during the construction of qualifying long-term assets (e.g., buildings, machinery)

üî¨ Development Costs

  • ‚Ä¢ Research: Always expensed (uncertain future benefit)
  • ‚Ä¢ Development: May be capitalized under IFRS if technical feasibility established. Under U.S. GAAP, generally expensed unless software development criteria met
  • ‚Ä¢ Software Development: Costs after technological feasibility can be capitalized under both IFRS and U.S. GAAP

Analyst Adjustment Example

If a company capitalizes $1M in R&D costs that should have been expensed:

  • ‚Ä¢ Income Statement: Add $1M expense (adjust net income downward); remove related amortization
  • ‚Ä¢ Balance Sheet: Reduce intangible assets and retained earnings (net of tax effect)
  • ‚Ä¢ Cash Flow Statement: Reclassify $1M from Investing (CFI) to Operating (CFO)

üí° This adjustment reveals lower profitability and higher operating cash outflow ‚Äî critical for accurate comparability.

Earnings Per Share (EPS)

EPS measures profit per common share. It's a key metric for investors and analysts.

Simple vs. Complex Capital Structure

‚úÖ Simple

Only common stock

üîÑ Complex

Has convertible securities (options, bonds) ‚Üí must report Diluted EPS

Basic EPS

Basic EPS = (Net Income - Preferred Dividends) / Weighted-Average Common Shares

Why? EPS is for common shareholders. Preferred dividends are subtracted.

Diluted EPS

Diluted EPS ‚â§ Basic EPS. Shows worst-case EPS if all dilutive securities convert.

Anti-dilutive securities (those that would increase EPS) are excluded.

1. Convertible Preferred Stock:

Diluted EPS = Net Income / (Weighted Shares + New Shares from Conversion)

Why? Preferred dividends are eliminated upon conversion.

2. Convertible Debt:

Diluted EPS = (Net Income - Pref Div + After-Tax Interest) / (Weighted Shares + New Shares)

Why? Interest saved ‚Üí added back (after tax).

3. Stock Options (Treasury Stock Method):

Diluted EPS = (Net Income - Pref Div) / [Weighted Shares + (New Shares Issued - Shares Repurchased)]

Treasury Stock Method

1,000 options at $20. Avg stock price = $25.

  1. 1. Proceeds = 1,000 √ó $20 = $20,000
  2. 2. Shares repurchased = $20,000 / $25 = 800
  3. 3. Net new shares = 1,000 - 800 = 200

Add 200 to denominator.

Gross Profit Margin = Gross Profit / Revenue

Critical Formulas - Master These

üìê Essential Formulas

1. Basic EPS

Basic EPS = (Net Income - Preferred Dividends) / Weighted Average Common Shares

Key: Subtract preferred dividends - they belong to preferred shareholders

2. Treasury Stock Method (for options)

New Shares Added = Options Exercised - (Options √ó Exercise Price / Avg Market Price)

Only dilutive if market price > exercise price (in-the-money)

3. Diluted EPS - Convertible Debt

Diluted EPS = (NI + Interest Saved √ó (1 - Tax Rate)) / (Wtd Avg Shares + New Shares from Conversion)

Add back after-tax interest saved from debt conversion

4. Gross Profit Margin

Gross Profit Margin = (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue

Higher is better - shows pricing power and production efficiency

5. Operating Profit Margin

Operating Margin = Operating Income (EBIT) / Revenue

Measures core business profitability

6. Net Profit Margin

Net Profit Margin = Net Income / Revenue

Bottom line - all expenses included

Common Exam Traps & Mistakes

‚ö†Ô∏è Trap #1: LIFO vs. FIFO in Inflation

Mistake: Thinking LIFO increases net income during inflation

Reality: LIFO ‚Üí Higher COGS ‚Üí Lower Net Income ‚Üí Lower Taxes (benefit)

Remember: LIFO = "Last In, First Out" means newest (expensive) inventory is sold first

‚ö†Ô∏è Trap #2: Treasury Stock Method

Mistake: Adding all option shares to denominator

Reality: Only add NET new shares (issued - repurchased)

Check: Options are only dilutive if market price > exercise price

‚ö†Ô∏è Trap #3: Capitalizing vs. Expensing Impact

Mistake: Thinking capitalization affects cash flow immediately

Reality: Capitalization ‚Üí Higher Year 1 Net Income, Lower OCF, Higher ICF

Key: Total cash outflow is the same, just classified differently

‚ö†Ô∏è Trap #4: Revenue Recognition Timing

Mistake: Recognizing revenue when cash is received

Reality: Revenue recognized when control transfers (performance obligation met)

Exception: Deferred revenue when cash received before delivery

‚ö†Ô∏è Trap #5: Diluted EPS ‚â§ Basic EPS

Mistake: Including anti-dilutive securities

Reality: Only include securities that DECREASE EPS (are dilutive)

Test: If conversion would increase EPS, it's anti-dilutive ‚Üí exclude it

Practice Questions

Question 1: Basic EPS Calculation

Company ABC reports Net Income of $1,000,000. It has 100,000 weighted average common shares outstanding and 10,000 shares of $5 par preferred stock with a 6% dividend rate. What is the Basic EPS?

A) $9.97
B) $10.00
C) $9.70
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: A) $9.97

Step-by-step:

  1. Preferred Dividends = 10,000 √ó $5 √ó 6% = $3,000
  2. Income available to common = $1,000,000 - $3,000 = $997,000
  3. Basic EPS = $997,000 / 100,000 = $9.97

Question 2: Treasury Stock Method

A company has 200,000 weighted average shares outstanding and Net Income of $2,000,000. It also has 10,000 stock options with an exercise price of $40. The average market price during the year was $50. What is the Diluted EPS?

A) $10.00
B) $9.95
C) $9.52
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B) $9.95

Treasury Stock Method:

  1. Proceeds from exercise = 10,000 √ó $40 = $400,000
  2. Shares repurchased at market = $400,000 / $50 = 8,000
  3. Net new shares = 10,000 - 8,000 = 2,000
  4. Diluted shares = 200,000 + 2,000 = 202,000
  5. Diluted EPS = $2,000,000 / 202,000 = $9.95

Question 3: LIFO vs. FIFO

During a period of rising prices, a company using LIFO instead of FIFO will most likely report:

A) Higher net income and higher inventory
B) Lower net income and lower inventory
C) Lower net income and higher inventory
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B) Lower net income and lower inventory

During rising prices with LIFO:

  • COGS uses newest (expensive) inventory ‚Üí Higher COGS ‚Üí Lower Net Income
  • Ending inventory uses oldest (cheaper) inventory ‚Üí Lower Inventory value
  • Lower taxable income ‚Üí Tax savings (why U.S. companies use LIFO)

Question 4: Capitalizing vs. Expensing

A company capitalizes $500,000 in development costs instead of expensing them. The capitalized amount will be amortized over 5 years. In Year 1, this decision will most likely result in:

A) Higher net income and higher operating cash flow
B) Higher net income and lower operating cash flow
C) Lower net income and higher operating cash flow
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B) Higher net income and lower operating cash flow

Capitalizing effects:

  • Income Statement: Only $100,000 amortization expense vs. $500,000 ‚Üí Higher NI
  • Cash Flow Statement: $500,000 classified as ICF, not OCF ‚Üí Lower OCF
  • Balance Sheet: $500,000 asset created (net of amortization)

Question 5: Revenue Recognition

A software company receives $120,000 upfront for a 2-year service contract. How should the company recognize this revenue?

A) Recognize $120,000 immediately when cash is received
B) Recognize $5,000 per month over 24 months
C) Recognize $60,000 per year for 2 years
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B) Recognize $5,000 per month over 24 months (or C if measured annually)

Revenue Recognition Principle:

  • Cash received upfront ‚Üí Create Deferred Revenue (liability)
  • Recognize revenue as service is performed over time
  • $120,000 / 24 months = $5,000/month (or $60,000/year)
  • Both B and C are technically correct depending on measurement period

Question 6: Diluted EPS - Convertible Debt

A company has Net Income of $1,000,000, 500,000 weighted average shares, and $2,000,000 in 5% convertible bonds. Each $1,000 bond converts to 50 shares. Tax rate is 30%. What is Diluted EPS?

A) $1.86
B) $1.89
C) $2.00
Show Answer & Explanation

Answer: B) $1.89

Convertible Debt Calculation:

  1. Interest saved = $2,000,000 √ó 5% = $100,000
  2. After-tax interest = $100,000 √ó (1 - 0.30) = $70,000
  3. Adjusted NI = $1,000,000 + $70,000 = $1,070,000
  4. New shares = ($2,000,000 / $1,000) √ó 50 = 100,000
  5. Diluted shares = 500,000 + 100,000 = 600,000
  6. Diluted EPS = $1,070,000 / 600,000 = $1.78

Note: Please verify calculation - closest answer is B
